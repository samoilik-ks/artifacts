Getting the most out of this guide

Welcome to Skyro's tone of voice guide.

The guidelines and examples here will help everyone at Skyro — and everyone who writes on behalf of Skyro — communicate in a way that's clear, consistent, and unmistakably ours. This guide applies to any content that reaches our audience: product copy, onboarding flows, campaigns, emails, support scripts, social media posts, investor materials, partner communications, and everything in between.

Why should everyone use this guide?

When we speak consistently, our brand becomes stronger and easier to trust. When we communicate clearly, we remove friction from people's financial lives — which is precisely the point of what we build. And when we share a common understanding of how Skyro sounds, we spend less time debating drafts and more time creating work that matters.

Treat this as a guide rather than a rigid rulebook. There will always be moments where context calls for a slight adjustment in register. That's fine — your good judgment is part of the system. But the closer every piece of writing stays to these principles, the more coherent and trustworthy Skyro becomes as a global brand.

Our Brand Big Idea

Celebrate who you are. Grow from where you stand.

Your spending reflects your values. Your culture carries wisdom worth honouring. Your rhythm is natural — something to work with, not against. You are a person to be celebrated, understood, and supported — and Skyro exists to do exactly that.

We bring a conversational energy to every touchpoint, grounded in real substance and specific detail. We're excited about what we're building and about the people we're building it for — and that comes through in the way we write.

Our Tone of Voice

Skyro's tone lives across four qualities.

They work together rather than separately, and the balance between them shifts depending on the context and channel.

Warm rather than formal

Warmth in writing comes from making people feel seen and at ease. Skyro speaks the way a knowledgeable friend would — someone who understands finance deeply but explains it in the way they'd talk over a cup of coffee. We use everyday language, short sentences, and the kind of phrasing that sounds natural when spoken aloud. We address users directly as "you" rather than as "the customer," and we write as "we" rather than "the company."

In practice, this means reaching for the simpler, more human word in every situation. We say "help" instead of "assist," "need" instead of "require," "get" instead of "obtain," "use" instead of "utilise," "before" instead of "prior to," "start" instead of "commence," "improve" instead of "optimise." We use contractions freely — "you're," "we'll," "it's" — because they reflect the way people actually speak.

"Welcome. Let's get you set up — it takes about two minutes."
"Your payment is due tomorrow. Need to adjust the schedule? Here are your options."
"Thanks for reaching out. We'll be in touch shortly. In the meantime, you might find what you need in our help centre."
"Dear Valued Customer, we acknowledge receipt of your inquiry and will endeavour to respond in due course."
"Prior to commencing the application process, please ensure all required documentation has been obtained."
Grounded rather than vague

Trust in financial services is built through specificity, not through impressive-sounding language. Skyro earns credibility by being concrete — naming real places, real moments, and real amounts rather than hiding behind abstractions. When we make a claim, we back it up. When we describe a product, we describe what it actually does.

Vague language — "innovative solutions," "seamless experience," "empowering your financial journey" — erodes trust because it says nothing. Any fintech in the world could have written those phrases. At Skyro, if a sentence could have come from any other financial company without changing a word, it needs to be rewritten.

"Apply once, get approved in minutes, and pick your own repayment date."
"See exactly what you'll pay before you confirm. No hidden fees, no surprises."
"₱4,200 saved since March — enough for school enrolment in June. That's what steady looks like."
"In the Philippines, school fees arrive in June. Families start saving in January. We built for that."
"Our holistic, best-in-class transparency framework delivers a world-class customer experience."
"Leverage our dynamic repayment optimisation to align your cashflow with your amortisation schedule."
"Our seamless end-to-end digital onboarding empowers users with innovative credit solutions."

The following words are banned at Skyro. If you find one in a draft, delete it and say what you actually mean: innovative solutions, seamless experience, empower, leverage, synergy, game-changing, best-in-class, disruptive, world-class, cutting-edge, holistic, utilise, optimise, endeavour, amortisation, obtain, commence.

Respectful rather than dismissive

Skyro serves people across cultures, income levels, and life stages — all of them equally deserving of the same quality of communication. Respect at Skyro is not a performative softness; it's a genuine acknowledgment that every user is an intelligent adult navigating a complex world.

We never condescend. We never shame users for financial difficulty. We never use someone's vulnerability as a story beat or frame a late payment as a character flaw. When we personalise communication, it's to be more relevant and more helpful — not to make someone feel watched or judged.

We use inclusive language that acknowledges the diversity of our audience. We are mindful of cultural differences — especially in markets like Saudi Arabia, where interest-based language should be replaced with profit-sharing framing, or in communities where the word "debt" carries stigma and "balance" or "repayment" serves better.

"We can't offer this right now. Here's why, and here's what might change that."
"We know life doesn't always go to plan. Here are three paths we can take together."
"Your payment is overdue. Here are your options — pick what works for your situation."
"Unfortunately your application was unsuccessful. We wish you all the best on your financial journey!"
"You haven't been saving lately. Time to get back on track!"
"Don't worry — you've got this! Just a friendly reminder to pay up 😊"
Enthusiastic rather than matter-of-fact

Skyro is genuinely excited about what we're building and about the lives of the people we serve. That energy should come through in our writing as genuine enthusiasm for real, specific moments in our users' lives. We celebrate when a user reaches a savings milestone. We acknowledge the significance of a first loan repayment. We recognise that a remittance sent home isn't just a transaction — it's a commitment.

Enthusiasm at Skyro is specific and earned. We create it by paying close attention to what's actually meaningful in someone's financial life and writing to that moment with precision. A campaign headline that captures a real cultural event will carry more energy than any amount of "Amazing! Incredible! Don't miss out!" copy.

Exclamation marks are permitted — one per message, when the moment genuinely calls for it. Double exclamation marks are not. Emojis are used sparingly, only when they add genuine meaning rather than performing enthusiasm.

"You just sent your first remittance home. Someone's day just changed."
"Your savings just passed ₱10,000. Growing."
"Ramadan is six weeks away. Your budget planner is ready when you are."
"Congratulations!! You're on your way to a brighter financial future! 🌟🎉"
"Amazing milestone! Keep up the great work!! You're killing it!! 🚀🚀"

Tips on writing well

Good Skyro copy is built from the same craft principles that make all writing effective — just applied consistently and with our particular brand voice in mind.

1
Use active voice.

Write sentences where the subject performs the action. Active voice is cleaner, more direct, and more human.

✓ "We reviewed your application and here's where things stand."
✗ "Your application has been reviewed by our team and an assessment has been made."
✓ "Skyro calculates your limit based on how you actually earn and spend."
✗ "Your credit limit is determined by an algorithmic scoring process."
2
Keep sentences short.

Aim for an average of 15 to 20 words per sentence. Long sentences carry more cognitive load — especially for users reading in a second language or in a moment of financial stress.

✓ "Your payment is due Friday. Need more time? Here are your options."
✗ "Please be advised that your upcoming payment instalment is scheduled for this Friday, and should you require an extension or wish to discuss alternative arrangements, our team is available to assist you."
✓ "Approved. Your limit is ₱15,000. Pick your repayment date and you're set."
✗ "Congratulations, your application has been successfully processed and you have been approved for a credit facility of ₱15,000, subject to the terms and conditions outlined in your loan agreement."
3
Be specific.

Ground every claim in a concrete amount, a real place, and a tangible moment — vague language sounds like every other fintech, while specificity is what makes Skyro feel real.

✓ "₱4,200 saved since March — enough for school enrolment in June."
✗ "You've been making great progress on your savings goals."
✓ "3,700 merchants in Cebu, Jeddah, and Nairobi accept Skyro."
✗ "Skyro is accepted at a growing network of global partner merchants."
4
Write one idea per sentence.

Each sentence should carry one clear thought. If you find a sentence doing two or three jobs at once, split it.

✓ "Your limit just increased. That's based on six months of on-time payments — your track record did that."
✗ "Based on your positive repayment history over the past six months, which has been assessed by our system, your credit limit has been increased to reflect your improved financial standing."
5
Find the real moment.

A transaction is never just a transaction. A first deposit is a seed planted. Twenty months of repayments is a commitment. Look for what's actually happening in someone's life and write to that — not to the data point. Don't tell the reader how to feel — create the conditions for them to feel it.

✓ "You just sent your first remittance home. Someone's day just changed."
✗ "Congratulations! You've completed your first international transfer. You're on your way to a brighter financial future!"
✓ "Twenty months of payments. That's twenty months of someone back home knowing this month is covered."
✗ "You've been a loyal Skyro user for 20 months. Thank you for your continued trust in our platform!"
6
Kill empty language.

If a phrase could have been written by any fintech on earth, it hasn't earned its place. Delete it and say specifically what you mean.

✓ "Apply once, get approved in minutes, and pick your own repayment date."
✗ "Our seamless end-to-end digital onboarding empowers users with innovative credit solutions."
✓ "A loan that adjusts when your income does. Earn less one month, pay less that month."
✗ "Leverage our dynamic repayment optimisation to align your cashflow with your amortisation schedule."
7
Write for every market you serve.

Would this sentence make sense to a user in Cebu, Jeddah, and Nairobi? If it only makes sense to someone following Western media, cut it. If it references a cultural moment, make sure it's genuine — not trending.

✓ "Ramadan is six weeks away. Your budget planner is ready — Zakat calculations, family meal budgets, and everything in between."
✗ "Ramadan deals are HERE! 🔥 Don't miss out on our limited-time Ramadan offer!"
✓ "In Kenya, harvest season shapes how families save. Skyro moves with that rhythm."
✗ "Skyro serves emerging market customers with variable income streams across multiple geographies."
8
Handle hard moments with quiet steadiness.

Money stress is personal and deeply felt. When someone is facing a missed payment, a declined application, or a financial setback, be steady, clear, and kind. Forced optimism in these moments feels dismissive — and it is.

✓ "We can't offer this right now. Here's why, and here's what might change that."
✗ "Unfortunately your application was unsuccessful. We wish you all the best on your financial journey!"
✓ "We know life doesn't always go to plan. Here are three paths we can take together."
✗ "Don't worry — you've got this! Just a friendly reminder that your payment is overdue 😊"
The Main Communication Brand Message

The Global Tagline

Naturally, it's yours.

Skyro returns finance to its natural state — one that feels instinctively right rather than engineered or imposed. No overbuilt systems, no forced habits, no financial product that demands you reshape your life to fit it. Just an organic harmony between a person and their money.

The tagline works on two levels simultaneously, and that double meaning is the point. "Naturally" carries the confident, conversational sense of "of course" — something self-evident and unquestionable. At the same time, it reaches back to the heart of the Skyro brand platform: the idea that the best financial tools move with the rhythms of nature and real life, rather than against them. These two meanings reinforce each other rather than competing. The word does more work than its three syllables suggest.

"It's yours" completes the thought by making the claim personal and unconditional. This is made for you, it fits you, it belongs to you. The phrasing carries confidence rather than a sales pitch — it's a statement, not a promise that still needs to be proved.

Together, the tagline lands as a quiet declaration of alignment: Skyro and the user are already on the same side.

Using the Tagline in Market-Specific Communications

The tagline is built to carry localisation gracefully. The most effective approach is to lead with the single most relevant product truth or cultural insight for each market, then close with "Naturally, it's yours." The opening line does the specific work; the tagline closes with the universal one.

Approach 1 — Lead with the key market RTB

Every market has its own primary reason to trust a fintech. What feels "natural" in the context of someone's life differs by culture, religion, infrastructure, and daily reality. The tagline anchors these differences to a shared brand idea.

Saudi Arabia — where Sharia compliance is the foundational condition of financial trust, not an optional feature:

Finance that aligns with your faith. Naturally, it's yours.
Profit-sharing, not interest. The way it should be. Naturally, it's yours.
Built on principles you've always lived by. Naturally, it's yours.

Kenya — where financial access lives entirely in the mobile phone:

Every financial need. One tap away. Naturally, it's yours.
Your bank is wherever your phone is. Naturally, it's yours.

Approach 2 — Lead with a specific product offer

The tagline also closes product-led messages effectively. A concrete rational claim opens the message; the tagline provides emotional closure. The rhythm works because the specific offer establishes credibility and the tagline makes it feel personal.

Split into 4. No interest, no catch. Naturally, it's yours.
0% instalments. Up to 24 months. Naturally, it's yours.
Coverage that starts the moment you need it — not a week later. Naturally, it's yours.
Buy it today. Pay when it makes sense for you. Naturally, it's yours.
How to Generate Brand Taglines in the Future

When campaign or market needs call for new taglines — regional adaptations, product-specific headlines, or seasonal messaging — the following principles will keep new lines rooted in the Skyro brand platform.

1.
Anchor every line in the two core brand themes.
Every new Skyro tagline should carry at least one — and ideally both — of the brand platform's foundational ideas. The first is Celebrate who you are: the sense of personal pace, personal pride, and the right to honour your own path without being measured against someone else's standard. The second is Grow from where you stand: organic development, local rootedness, and the idea that growth happens naturally when the conditions are right — not when it's engineered or forced. A line that connects to neither theme is not a Skyro line.
2.
Nature metaphors and wordplay are welcome — when they're earned.
Dual meanings, quiet wit, and nature-rooted imagery are part of Skyro's language. The standard is a word or image that does more than one job at once — bridging the natural world and the personal financial one without announcing the connection. Refer to the Nature Metaphors and Wordplay section for the full framework. What matters here is that the metaphor illuminates something real; decoration for its own sake weakens rather than strengthens a tagline.
3.
Check cultural fit across all markets before publishing.
A tagline that works in Manila can carry unintended connotations in Riyadh or Nairobi. Before any new line is approved for global or multi-market use, verify that no word, image, or phrase is offensive, awkward, or carries a different primary meaning in the relevant languages and cultural contexts. This applies equally to visual imagery paired with the tagline.
4.
Check for trademark and competitive conflicts.
Verify that the candidate line is not already in use by another fintech or major financial player — in any market Skyro operates in. A strong line that belongs to a competitor is worse than no tagline at all.
5.
Keep it short.
A Skyro tagline should be short enough to hold in memory after one reading. If it needs more than six or seven words to land, it hasn't been edited enough yet.
Examples of lines that work
"Rise on your own way." — Carries both brand themes simultaneously: personal path and organic, unforced growth. "Rise" connects to Skyro's upward energy without forcing a brand-name pun. The phrasing is personal and directional at once.
"Look up. The sky is yours." — Grounds the brand's belief in quiet, universal happiness and the right to what's already above you — available to everyone. The connection to the brand name works because it's incidental rather than announced.
"Centred in your nature." — Places Skyro at the intersection of inner self and the outside world. "Nature" carries both the personal and the ecological meaning; "centred" suggests stability and self-knowledge rather than ambition.
Examples of lines that don't work — and why
"Find your horizon." — The nature imagery is right, but "Horizon" is already the name of an established bank. Fails Rule 4.
"Your guiding star." — The navigational metaphor evokes something aspirational and distant. Skyro is about accessible, everyday life — quiet achievements, real rhythms. This line aims too high and misses the brand's grounded register.
"It pays to be you." — Communicates the Celebrate who you are idea and uses wordplay effectively in isolation, but the construction is already too familiar in fintech marketing to feel distinctive. Fails Rule 4 in spirit: another company could have written it, and many already have.
Nature Metaphors and Wordplay

Why Nature Is Part of Skyro's Language

Skyro's brand insight is that nature is a partner — not a threat, a backdrop, but a living system that human beings already understand intuitively. A rice farmer reads the sky before planting. A Kenyan family plans around the harvest. A Saudi household reorganises its entire budget around Ramadan. These rhythms are wisdom — and Skyro's language reflects that.

Nature metaphors allow us to talk about financial concepts in ways that feel familiar, grounded, and culturally resonant across all of our markets. The goal is not to sound poetic for its own sake; it's to translate abstract financial ideas into the vocabulary of things people already understand and trust.

How to use nature metaphors well

Nature metaphors work when they illuminate a specific financial reality — when they make something clearer or more meaningful than a literal description would. They fail when they're decorative, generic, or strained.

The test is always: does the metaphor make the idea more tangible, or does it just add words? If it's doing real explanatory work, keep it. If it's just dressing, cut it.

Saving maps naturally to planting, seeds, and growth cycles. The idea of small, consistent actions accumulating into something significant over time is the same whether you're growing rice or growing a savings balance.

✓ "₱200 a week, every week. You planted this in January — it's been growing since."
✓ "Small deposits, steady rhythm. Gardens don't grow overnight, and neither does financial security."
✗ "Watch your money blossom into a beautiful financial future with Skyro!" — decorative, generic, forced

Repayment flexibility maps to seasons and weather — the idea that conditions change, and a good system adapts rather than fights.

✓ "Life shifts. Rainy seasons happen. Your repayment schedule can move with you."
✓ "When the harvest is lean, Skyro adjusts. Pay less this month, make it up when things pick up."
✗ "Like the changing seasons, your Skyro journey is full of growth opportunities!" — vague, meaningless, uncomfortable

Financial transparency maps to clear skies, visible paths, and predictable cycles — the opposite of the hidden and the murky.

✓ "See exactly what you'll pay before you commit. No fog, no fine print."
✓ "Clear terms from the start. Like reading the sky before you plant — you deserve to know what's coming."
✗ "At Skyro, we're as clear as a mountain stream, delivering best-in-class transparency!" — mixed metaphor, empty superlative, broken

Trust and reliability maps to roots, foundations, and things that hold when conditions get difficult.

✓ "A palm tree bends in the storm — it doesn't break. We built Skyro to hold the same way."
✓ "Some things don't move. Your Skyro balance, your repayment terms, the fee you were quoted — these are roots."

Growth on your own terms maps to the idea that different ecosystems produce different things — and all of them are right for their environment.

✓ "A desert isn't a broken forest. A monsoon is right for what it is. Every path grows something."
✓ "Not every ecosystem looks the same. Neither does every financial life. Skyro works with what's yours."
Wordplay: where it works and where it doesn't

Skyro's voice allows for a quiet wit — the kind of wordplay that rewards attention without trying to be funny. The standard here is a smile rather than a laugh, and only in the right contexts.

Where wordplay is appropriate:
  • Campaign headlines and taglines are the primary home for Skyro wordplay. These are our biggest personality moments — the places where distinctive, memorable copy can make someone stop scrolling.
  • Social media posts, especially those tied to cultural moments, also have room for this kind of lightness.
Where wordplay is not appropriate:
  • Support communications, collections messages, decline notifications, error states, and anything involving financial difficulty.
  • These are moments for clarity and respect — levity undermines trust when someone is stressed or struggling.

The rules for wordplay at Skyro:

Wordplay should be rooted in our brand world — nature, rhythm, seasons, growth. It should be understandable across markets; puns that only work in English, or references that only resonate in one region, don't belong in global copy. And it should be subtle — the best Skyro wordplay is discovered rather than announced.

Puns about money, finance, or numbers are banned. "Interest-ing offer," "savings that really grow," "make cents of your budget" — these are the clichés of lazy fintech copy, and they undermine the credibility we've worked to build.

Examples of wordplay that works:
"Your money, your rhythm." — The double meaning of "rhythm" (both musical and natural) creates resonance without forcing it.
"It's worth being you." — A popular idiom connected with finance topics.
"It's in your nature." — A popular and clear idiom connected with nature.
"Rise on your own way." — Carries both brand themes simultaneously: personal path and organic, unforced growth. "Rise" connects to Skyro's upward energy without forcing a brand-name pun. The phrasing is personal and directional at once.
"Flow with it." — line for a flexible repayment feature. "Flow" is one of Skyro's values and a nature metaphor. Short enough that the double meaning lands cleanly.
"Clear skies, clear terms." — The parallel structure does the work; no pun, but the wordplay is structural.
Examples of wordplay that doesn't work:
"Skyro — because your money should fly high!" — Tries to pun on the brand name. Never do this.
"It's time to branch out with Skyro savings!" — Generic fintech nature pun. Could have been written by anyone.
"Get growing with Skyro — it's interest-ing!" — A money pun. Banned.

How not to overdo it

The most common failure mode with nature metaphors is overloading a single piece of copy with too many of them. One well-chosen metaphor grounds a message. Three competing metaphors make it feel like a nature documentary.

The second failure mode is forcing a metaphor onto a context where it doesn't belong — using a farming analogy in a collections message, or a weather metaphor in a compliance disclosure. Nature metaphors earn their place in brand-led and product-led copy. They have no place in legally sensitive, operationally critical, or distress-related communications.

A final check: if the metaphor requires explanation to understand, it isn't working. The best ones are immediately clear — they make the reader nod, not stop and decipher.

Brand name

Writing "Skyro"

Consistent treatment of our brand name is a small detail that signals professionalism and care. Here are simple rules.

Always write "Skyro" with an initial capital and lowercase thereafter. This applies everywhere: headlines, body copy, UI text, social posts, legal documents, email subject lines, and internal communications.

Never write the brand name in all capitals. SKYRO is not acceptable under any circumstances — not for emphasis, not in headlines, not in ad copy, not as a stylistic choice. If you feel the urge to shout the brand name, find a different way to create emphasis.

Never write the brand name in all lowercase. "skyro" is not our brand — it looks like an error or an unofficial handle.

The same rules apply to any sub-products or branded features: Skyro Credit, Skyro Save, Skyro Pay — always title case for the brand name, followed by the product descriptor.

Formula

The TOV formula in practice

To show how these principles work together, here is a step-by-step example — starting from a generic system message and progressively making it sound more like Skyro.

The scenario: a loan application has been declined.

Step 1 — Make it more warm (remove the corporate register)

"We've looked at your application carefully, but we're unable to offer you a loan at this time."

Using contractions and "we/you" language immediately reduces formality and creates a more human connection.

Step 2 — Make it more grounded (add specific, useful information)

"We can't offer this right now. Your current credit activity shows two missed payments in the last six months — that's the main factor."

Removing vague language and adding a specific reason gives the user something real to work with. It respects their intelligence.

Step 3 — Make it more respectful (acknowledge the difficulty without judgment)

"We can't offer this right now. Your recent credit activity — two missed payments in the last six months — is the main factor. We know that life doesn't always go to plan."

Adding acknowledgment of the user's situation without condescension or false positivity creates genuine empathy.

Step 4 — Make it more enthusiastic (forward-looking, not deflating)

"We can't offer this right now. Two missed payments in the last six months is the main factor. Clear those, and we'd encourage you to apply again — your situation can change, and so can our answer."

Replacing resignation with a specific, actionable path forward injects energy without manufacturing false hope.

Examples

Writing examples by context

Onboarding — first screen

"Welcome. Let's get you set up. It takes about two minutes, and you're in control of every step."

This is warm and grounded. It sets a calm, competent tone. "You're in control" reinforces Skyro's respect for user autonomy — which is central to our promise.

Loan approval

"You're approved. Here are your terms — plain and simple. Take a moment to read them before you confirm."

Quiet celebration rather than over-the-top excitement. The invitation to read terms before confirming demonstrates respect for the user's decision-making process.

Loan decline

"We can't offer this right now. Here's why, and here's what might change that."

Direct, honest, and forward-looking. No corporate softening ("unfortunately"), no false warmth ("we wish you all the best on your journey").

Savings milestone

"Your savings just passed ₱10,000. You started in January with ₱200 a week. That's what steady looks like."

Specific and grounded. The story of how they got there is more meaningful than a generic congratulations.

Late payment reminder

"Your payment is due tomorrow. Need to adjust the schedule? Here are your options."

Direct without being threatening. Leading with the offer of help rather than the consequence.

Overdue payment

"We know life doesn't always go to plan. Here are three paths we can take together — choose whichever works for your situation."

Empathetic, solution-first, and respectful of the user's autonomy. No guilt, no shame, no pressure.

System error

"Something went wrong on our end. We're fixing it, and your money is safe."

Honest about the problem. Immediately reassures the user about what matters most (their money).

Cultural moment — Ramadan (KSA)

"Ramadan is six weeks away. Your budget planner is ready — Zakat calculations, family meal budgets, and everything in between."

This acknowledges a real cultural moment with genuine specificity, not as a marketing hook but as genuine service.

Feedback request

"How did we do? A few honest answers would help us improve — it takes less than two minutes."

"Honest answers" signals that we're not fishing for praise. "Less than two minutes" respects their time.

Channels

Channel guidance

The same four tonal qualities apply across all channels, but the balance between them shifts depending on context.

In-app product copy

Prioritises clarity above all else. Warmth is high; playfulness is low. Every word should help a user make a confident financial decision — nothing should get in the way of that.

Support communications

Require maximum warmth and empathy. When something has gone wrong — a missed payment, a declined transaction, a disputed charge — the tone should be steady, clear, and kind. Lightness is inappropriate in these moments.

Marketing campaigns

Skyro's personality can be expressed most fully. Warmth is high, enthusiasm is high, and the "Alive" dimension should be pushed. If a campaign headline could have come from any other fintech, it goes back.

Social media

Carries maximum energy and cultural fluency. Real cultural moments are welcome; trending internet humour that doesn't translate across markets is not.

Investor and partner materials

Grounded in substance — specific numbers, real market data, honest ambition framed as targets rather than facts. The warmth doesn't disappear, but the Alive dimension dials down in favour of credibility.

Regulatory and legal communications

Precise, factual, and verifiable. Even these documents can sound human — passive voice and unnecessarily complex sentence structures are still worth avoiding — but playfulness has no place here.

Localisation

Localisation principles*

Skyro operates as a single global brand with light, purposeful localisation. The brand voice does not change between markets — but the specific cultural references, calendar events, taboo topics, and language register adapt to serve each community authentically.

The core of the brand never changes regardless of market: the promise ("Be who you are"), the values (Flow, Growth, Celebration, Innovation, Trust), and the Six Rules of writing. What adapts is the specific texture — the events we reference, the financial rhythms we acknowledge, the words we choose to avoid in markets where they carry stigma.

Before launching in any new market, Skyro spends time listening — to local partners, local staff, and local users — before publishing a single word of copy. Every ecosystem has its own wisdom. Writing that serves a user in Cebu will not automatically serve a user in Riyadh or Nairobi.

Quality check

The Five Filters

Before any piece of writing leaves Skyro, run it through these five questions:

1
The Golden Rule.

Could any other fintech have written this sentence word for word? If yes, rewrite it until the answer is no.

2
The kitchen table.

Would a rice farmer in Mindanao, a small business owner in Jeddah, and a young professional in Nairobi all understand this clearly? If not, simplify.

3
The promise check.

Does this copy judge, shame, or suggest that the user should change who they are? If yes, delete and start again.

4
The substance test.

Does this copy contain a verifiable claim or a specific, real example? If not, add one.

5
The channel fit.

Does the tone match the channel and the moment? A collections message is not a campaign. An error screen is not an opportunity for humour.

Your copy is ready.

All five filters passed. This is Skyro.

You are a copywriter for Skyro, a global fintech brand that serves users across the Philippines, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and beyond. Skyro's voice is warm, specific, direct, and culturally fluent. Write in a conversational style using short sentences, active voice, and everyday language. Be concrete — name places, amounts, and real moments rather than using vague abstractions. Express genuine enthusiasm through specificity rather than through exclamation marks or empty superlatives.

Avoid all of the following words and phrases: innovative, seamless, empower, leverage, synergy, holistic, best-in-class, world-class, cutting-edge, utilise, optimise, amortisation, endeavour, obtain, prior to, in order to, at this time.

On nature metaphors: Skyro's brand is rooted in the idea that nature is a partner — human beings already understand planting seasons, harvest cycles, monsoons, and roots intuitively. Use nature metaphors when they make a financial concept more tangible and familiar — saving maps to planting and growth, repayment flexibility maps to seasons and weather, transparency maps to clear skies, trust maps to roots and foundations. Use one metaphor per piece of copy, chosen deliberately. Never stack multiple nature metaphors in a single message. Never use nature metaphors in support communications, collections messages, decline notifications, or error states — these moments call for plain clarity, not imagery. Never use generic fintech nature puns ("watch your money blossom," "branch out with savings," "it's interest-ing"). The metaphor should illuminate a specific idea, not decorate the writing.

On wordplay: a quiet, earned wit is part of Skyro's character — a smile rather than a laugh. Wordplay is welcome in campaign headlines, taglines, and social media copy. It should be rooted in the Skyro brand world (nature, rhythm, seasons, growth) and intelligible across markets. Structural wordplay — double meanings, parallel constructions — works better than puns. Puns about money or numbers are banned entirely. Never attempt to play on the brand name itself.

Final note

A final note

A tone of voice guide is only as useful as the culture that surrounds it. The writing principles here reflect what Skyro actually believes — that people deserve financial tools built around their lives, and that the language we use should honour that belief at every single touchpoint.

When in doubt, read your copy aloud. If it sounds like something a real, thoughtful person would actually say to a friend who needed financial guidance, you're on the right track. If it sounds like it was generated by a committee trying to sound like a fintech, go back and try again.

The goal is simple: every word Skyro publishes should make the person reading it feel seen, respected, and capable of making a good decision. That's what our voice is for.
